## What tomorrow is about (the one-paragraph map)

Last week (s3) you built **one** decision tree — a nested if/else state machine that can overfit
(memorize the training data's noise). Tomorrow's whole session is one idea stretched four ways:
**many mediocre models, combined, beat one clever model.**
- **Bagging** — train many trees on random re-samples of the data, let them vote.
- **Random Forest** — bagging + each tree only sees a random *subset of features* (forces disagreement).
- **AdaBoost** — train models in sequence, each one focusing on what the previous got wrong.
- **Gradient Boosting** — same sequel idea, but each new model fits the *residual error* directly.

🔧 **Your hook:** it's ECC majority logic. One noisy bit-read lies; a vote across many reads doesn't.
RAID for models — redundancy converts many weak, *independent* signals into one reliable answer.
The magic word is **independent**: correlated trees = correlated failures (same as correlated dies).

## 3 questions to hold during the lecture (write the answers live)

1. **Why does randomness HELP here?** (Bagging deliberately throws away data per tree; forests
   deliberately hide features. When does adding noise make a system *more* reliable?)
2. **Bagging vs boosting — parallel vs serial?** (Which one can you train on 8 cores at once, and
   which is inherently one-after-another? What does that mean on edge hardware?)
3. **When does an ensemble LOSE to a single tree?** (Cost: N× inference compute + RAM — what does a
   100-tree forest cost on an MCU? Where's the decision boundary?)
