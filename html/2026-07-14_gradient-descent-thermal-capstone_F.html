Gradient Descent — teaching the thermal forecaster to fit itself

spine: temp = w·load + b · grade ★★★ rebuild-from-scratch
The thread
How a model goes from confidently wrong → right 🎯
😄 Every fresh model is as confidently wrong as a Delhi auto-driver quoting the fare to a tourist — "₹500, sir!" Then it looks at the meter (the data), realises the miss, and negotiates its way down, step by step, until the number is fair. That negotiation is gradient descent.

Your goal: predict SSD temperature from load with a straight line — the R2 forecaster brick of your thermal-throttle project. The whole chain:

a line (temp = w·load + b) → measure the miss (loss) → feel the downhill (gradient) → step down (update) → repeat → best fit
🔧 The dataset we'll fit (3 telemetry readings): load 1 → 40°C · load 2 → 55°C · load 3 → 70°C. The true line is temp = 15·load + 25 — but the model doesn't know that yet. It has to discover it.
Step 1 · the three objects
Prediction → residual → loss (don't mix them up)

Three things, in order — the trap is calling the miss the "prediction."

PREDICTION P = w·load + b the line's guess RESIDUAL r = P − observed the MISS (how far off) LOSS L = Σ r² / n total wrongness, ONE number
🍛 Loss = the "kitchen disaster" score. Your amma's recipe says 2 spoons of chilli; you dumped in the whole box. The residual is the gap; we square it because over-spicing and under-spicing are both ruined food — and squaring also punishes a huge blunder far more than a tiny one.
🪤 The trap: the residual is not the prediction. Guess (P) → miss (r) → total (L). Three separate objects. Fumbling this is the #1 backprop confusion.
Step 2 · the bowl
Loss is a landscape — and you're blindfolded in it
🥘 Picture yourself blindfolded inside a giant kadhai (wok). You can't see the bottom — but under your feet you can feel which way the floor tilts, so you shuffle downhill. Do it enough and you reach the lowest point without ever seeing it. Gravity does the thinking; you just follow the slope.

Every knob setting (w, b) has a height = how wrong it is. Plot height over all settings → a bowl. The bottom = the best fit. Below is a slice: loss vs w (with b held at its best). Drag w and watch the ball — the ball is your blindfolded self.

Minimum sits at w = 15 (loss 0). Far from 15 → steep wall → big push toward the bottom. Near 15 → nearly flat → tiny nudges. The bowl's steepness IS the gradient.

Step 3 · the gradient
The gradient = the tilt of the floor under your feet

The gradient is the slope of the loss w.r.t. each knob — it points uphill (toward MORE loss). For one reading, from the chain rule you already own:

∂L/∂w = 2(P − T)·load = 2 · miss · input ∂L/∂b = 2(P − T) = 2 · miss

= error × input. A big miss or a big load ⇒ a steep tilt ⇒ that knob gets a big shove. (For the whole dataset you just sum each reading's contribution.)

🪤 Sign trap: the gradient points UPhill. To reduce loss you walk the opposite way — that's the minus sign coming next. Read a negative ∂L/∂w as "floor drops to the right → step w up."
Step 4 · the step (by hand)
The update rule — one honest hand-worked step
w ← w − η · ∂L/∂w η (eta) = learning rate = step size

Take one reading: load = 2, observed temp = 55°C. Current guess w = 10, b = 5, η = 0.05.

P = 10·2 + 5 = 25 → we predicted 25°C, way too cold r = P − T = 25 − 55 = −30 → miss of −30 ∂L/∂w = 2(−30)(2) = −120 (negative → push w UP) ∂L/∂b = 2(−30) = −60 w ← 10 − 0.05·(−120) = 16 b ← 5 − 0.05·(−60) = 8 new P = 16·2 + 8 = 40 → 25 → 40, closer to 55 ✓

One step dragged the prediction 25 → 40. Not there yet — that's the point. You repeat, each step shrinking the miss. Why don't we overshoot to 55 in one go? tap

Step 5 · watch it converge ▶ interactive
Run the loop — watch the slope line tilt, step by step
🔧 The 3 readings we're fitting: load 1 → 40°C · load 2 → 55°C · load 3 → 70°C. The model starts as a dumb flat line (w = 0, b = 0) and must discover the true line temp = 15·load + 25 by tuning both knobs.

Press ▶ Step (or drag the scrubber). Step 1 is the biggest jump — the gradient is steepest when you're most wrong — then each step eases: w overshoots and drifts back toward 15 while b climbs toward 25, both homing on the fit. The full arithmetic for each step prints on the right.

💡 Where does the 2/3 in the gradient come from? The loss is a mean of squares: L = (1/3)(r1² + r2² + r3²). Differentiating pulls out two pieces — 2 from the square (d/dw of r² = 2·r·(dr/dw)) × 1/3 from averaging over the 3 readings = 2/3. And each residual rides on its own load (dr/dw = load), giving the r1·1 + r2·2 + r3·3. (10 readings → 2/10 · a plain sum instead of a mean → just 2.)

Step 6 · the η knob (what-if)
Learning rate: the one dial that makes or breaks it
🚆 η is your travel style. Too big = a Mumbai local at rush hour that flies past your stop and out the other side. Too small = your grandmother crossing the road — perfectly safe, but you'll grow a beard waiting. Just right = a veteran auto weaving smoothly to the exact doorstep. 😄

0.01loss 219🐢 crawls — safe but slow

0.20loss ~360,000💥 overshoots → diverges
💥 Why too-big doesn't just wobble — it EXPLODES. A step that overshoots the bottom lands on the opposite wall, which is steeper → the gradient there is bigger → the next step is bigger still → it climbs the walls and loss blows up exponentially to ∞. The fix becomes the failure. There's a hard threshold (for this bowl, η ≈ 0.21): below it converges, above it diverges — no coming back.
🔧 NAND framing: it's read-retry Vref stepping — nudge the read-reference voltage in giant steps and you overshoot the low-BER valley, land in a worse spot, overshoot harder, and never settle. Same as any control loop (thermal throttle / PID) with the gain cranked too high: it goes unstable and swings wider, not tighter.
Lock it: η too small = slow but safe — still arrives. η too big = each correction overshoots to a steeper spot, so errors compound to infinity — the model gets worse, not just slower. Nine times out of ten, "the model won't converge" is just η set too high.
Step 7 · land it
When to use it, the sheet, and where it goes next 📜

✅ Reach for gradient descent when you can't (or don't want to) solve for the best weights directly — which is always, for anything nonlinear or big: neural nets, logistic regression, deep models.

🚫 When the simpler tool wins: for a plain linear fit like this one, there's a closed-form answer (the "normal equations") that lands on the exact best line in one shot — no stepping. Use that when the model is linear and the data fits in memory. GD earns its keep the moment a closed-form stops existing (every real network).
🔧 Your project: this is literally what trains the R2 temperature forecaster — gradient descent tunes the weights that map telemetry → predicted °C, so firmware can throttle before the drive cooks, not after.
prediction P = w·load + b loss L = Σ (P − T)² / n gradient ∂L/∂w = 2·Σ(P−T)·load , ∂L/∂b = 2·Σ(P−T) update w ← w − η·∂L/∂w , b ← b − η·∂L/∂b
🧵 The thread forward: a neural net is just millions of these weights, and backpropagation is the chain rule handing each one its own ∂L/∂w so gradient descent can nudge it. Master this one bowl and you've mastered how every model on Earth learns — from your thermal line to GPT.
▶ numpy twin — run the whole thing in Jupyter 
import numpy as np, matplotlib.pyplot as plt
x=np.array([1,2,3.]); y=np.array([40,55,70.]); n=len(x)
w=b=0.0; eta=0.08; hist=[]
for _ in range(60):
    p=w*x+b; res=p-y
    w-=eta*np.mean(2*res*x); b-=eta*np.mean(2*res)
    hist.append(np.mean(res**2))
print("w,b ->",round(w,2),round(b,2),"  (true 15, 25)")
fig,ax=plt.subplots(1,2,figsize=(10,4))
ax[0].scatter(x,y); ax[0].plot(x,w*x+b,'orange'); ax[0].set_title('the fit')
ax[1].plot(hist); ax[1].set_title('loss per step'); ax[1].set_yscale('log')
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
