🧭 How we got here — the full road to the bell

This curve didn't fall from the sky. It's the final shape of a ladder we climbed rung by rung. Read this once and the whole chain should come back — each rung says what it is, why we needed it, and the numbers.

1 · Raw readings — the starting universe

We begin with a stream of read-latencies straight off the drive — just numbers, no story yet:

readings = [80, 120, 90, 110, 100] µs

On their own these can't answer anything useful — "Is this drive healthy? Predictable? Will it blow my QoS budget?" Everything else in this page is squeezed out of these five numbers. Each reading is one round-trip time of a read command.

↓ but a pile of numbers has no summary — so first, find the center

2 · Mean — the center of gravity

Add them up, divide by how many. This is the anchor every later step measures against.

mean = (80+120+90+110+100) / 5 = 500 / 5 = 100 µs

😄 The mean is the friend-group's average height: it tells you nothing about the giant or the gnome individually — but it's the line everyone stands next to to be measured. Useless alone (two totally different drives can share a mean), yet nothing downstream works without it.

⚠️ Trap to remember: the mean is not the biggest value. It's the balance point — the spot where the readings below it exactly counterweight the ones above.

↓ now ask: how far does each reading stray from that center?

3 · Deviations — "how weird is each reading?"

Deviation = value − mean, one per reading. Positive = above average, negative = below.

80 − 100 = −20 90 − 100 = −10 100 − 100 = 0 120 − 100 = +20 110 − 100 = +10

😄 Deviation = your personal "how weird am I" score. Shaq is +15 cm from the room average, Danny DeVito is −10 cm. Same room, opposite weird.

🔑 Built-in spell-check: deviations always sum to zero (−20+20−10+10+0 = 0) — that's literally what "balance point" means. If yours don't sum to 0, your mean is wrong. But that zero is also a problem: you can't just average the deviations to summarise spread — you'd always get 0.

↓ deviations cancel to zero — so we square them before averaging

4 · Variance — one number for the spread

Square each deviation (kills the signs so they stop cancelling), then average over n−1:

squares: (−20)²+(20)²+(−10)²+(10)²+0² = 400+400+100+100+0 = 1000 variance = 1000 / (5−1) = 1000 / 4 = 250 µs²

Why square, not just drop the minus sign? Two jobs at once: (a) it kills the cancellation, and (b) it punishes big misses extra — a deviation of 20 becomes 400, while a 10 becomes only 100. That's the L2 flavour (squaring) vs L1 (absolute value, which treats all gaps evenly).

The catch: variance is now in µs² — squared units. "The spread is 250 µs-squared" means nothing to a human. So one more step…

↓ un-square it to get back to real, readable units

5 · Standard deviation σ — the readable headline

Take the square root of variance → back to real µs:

σ = √variance = √250 ≈ 15.81 µs

Now you can say it like a human: "reads typically land at 100 ± 15.81 µs" — a single, units-correct number for "how far a reading usually strays from the mean." This is the headline you put on a datasheet or set a throttle threshold against. You can't do that with the deviation list (it cancels) or with variance (wrong units).

↓ here's why σ matters more than the mean ever could

6 · The twist — same mean, different σ (mean alone LIES)

Two drives, both averaging 100 µs. The mean calls them identical. σ exposes the truth:

Drive A (steady): [98,100,102,99,101] mean=100 σ ≈ 1.58 µs → 98–102 Drive B (jittery): [80,120,90,110,100] mean=100 σ ≈ 15.81 µs → 84–116

🔧 Identical average latency — but Drive A holds a tight 98–102 band (datacenter-happy, short tail, SLAs safe), while Drive B's reads could be 80 or 120. Drive B's fat σ is the QoS nightmare — the p99 tail blows out and you get the support ticket, even though the average looked perfect. The mean said "fine"; σ told the truth about the jitter.

↓ σ is still just a number — so let's DRAW it

7 · The bell — σ drawn as a shape 🔔

"±15.81" is abstract. The bell curve is simply σ made visible: the peak sits at the mean, and the width is set by σ.

small σ → a razor-thin SPIKE (steady drive, all reads jammed near the mean) big σ → a WIDE hill (jittery drive, reads sprawl far out)

The coloured bands below are the 68–95–99.7 rule: ±1σ holds ~68%, ±2σ ~95%, ±3σ ~99.7%. So ±1σ is not the outlier fence — about 1 in 3 readings normally falls outside ±1σ. Real outliers live beyond ±2σ / ±3σ. (That's why Drive B's 80 and 120, sitting inside ±2σ = 68–132, are normal jitter, not outliers — a true outlier would be a ~150 µs read way out in the dead tail.)

🚀 …and where the bell leads next

The bell isn't the destination — it's a hub the rest of the course plugs into. Each of these is "the bell + σ, reused":

→ z-score — "how many σ out is this reading?"

Take any reading, measure its distance from the mean in units of σ:

z = (value − mean) / σ Drive B, reading 80: z = (80 − 100) / 15.81 = −1.26 → 1.26σ BELOW the mean Drive B, reading 120: z = (120 − 100) / 15.81 = +1.26 → 1.26σ ABOVE the mean

The z-score is literally the band a dot lands in, turned into a number. It also makes different drives comparable: a z of +2 means "rare" whether σ was 1.58 or 15.81. This is the bridge from "raw µs" to "how unusual, on a universal scale."

→ Anomaly detection — flag the far tail

Set a fence at ±3σ. Anything beyond it is "off-distribution" — it almost never happens by chance (<0.3%), so when it does, something real is wrong.

🔧 A read at 160 µs on Drive B (z ≈ +3.8) isn't jitter — it's a signal: read-disturb, an ECC/LDPC retry, a thermal throttle kicking in. The bell turns "weird" into a precise, tunable trip-wire instead of a gut feeling.

→ Probability & Bayes — area under the curve

The area under a slice of the bell = the probability a reading lands in that range. The ±1σ slice covers ~68% of the area → "68% chance a read is within 84–116 µs." This is the same probability machinery that feeds into Bayes (your earlier session) — "given a reading, how likely is each cause?"

→ ML normalization — re-centre to mean 0, σ 1

Many models assume features are roughly bell-shaped (Gaussian) and behave badly when one feature swings in the thousands and another in tenths. Standardizing fixes it: subtract the mean, divide by σ — which is exactly the z-score, applied to every value.

standardized value = (value − mean) / σ → new mean 0, new σ 1

🔧 Edge-AI: this keeps a temperature channel (°C) and a voltage channel (mV) on equal footing before the model sees them — and ties straight back to the covariance/PCA work, where σ-scaling decides which directions look "big."

🎯 The thread: the same σ you built by hand (readings → mean → deviation → variance → √) becomes the universal ruler for "normal vs weird" — z-scores, anomaly fences, probabilities, and feature scaling all run on it. One number, computed once, reused everywhere downstream.
