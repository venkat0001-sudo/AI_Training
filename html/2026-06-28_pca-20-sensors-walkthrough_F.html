20 Sensors → 3 Numbers

A baby-step walkthrough of the heavy notebook cell (cell 4.6). Same flow you did on the 6-student example — just bigger. Tap the yellow blanks to reveal. Use ← → or the buttons.

Step 1. The problem (why we even bother)

An SSD controller logs 20 sensor channels every reading — temperature, voltages, latencies, queue depths… 20 numbers, hundreds of times a second.

Storing & processing all 20 costs SRAM, MAC operations, and milliwatts — the three things you never have to spare on an edge device.

The question this whole walkthrough answers:

👉 Do I really need all 20 numbers, or do a few carry the real story?

🔮 Predict: gut feeling — out of 20 sensors on one controller, how many do you think are truly independent stories vs just echoes of each other? We built the data so the answer is exactly 3. Read on to see why.

Step 2. The secret: 20 sensors, only 3 real "stories"

Here's the trick baked into the data. The 20 channels aren't 20 separate things. They're all mixtures of just 3 hidden drivers:

🔥 heat
⚙️ workload
🔋 voltage
→
20 sensor
channels

Every channel is some recipe like:

sensor_7 = 0.4·heat + 1.2·workload − 0.3·voltage + tiny noise

So even though your eyes see 20 numbers, the data only really moves in 3 directions — the 3 drivers. The other 17 "degrees of freedom" are illusions; they're locked to these 3.

🔮 Predict: if two sensors are both mostly "heat" channels, when one goes up the other will… go up too — they're correlated. That redundancy is exactly what we'll exploit.

Step 4. Covariance — "do these two move together?"

Exactly the idea you nailed on Math vs Physics. For every pair of channels, covariance asks: when one is above its mean, is the other usually above too?

+ big → move together (a tight tilted cigar)
≈ 0   → unrelated (a round blob)
− big → move opposite

Do this for all pairs of 20 channels and you fill a grid: a 20 × 20 covariance matrix.

cov = np.cov(X.T)  →  shape (20, 20)

Because so many channels share the same 3 drivers, this grid is full of big off-diagonal numbers — lots of redundancy. A truly independent set of sensors would have a near-empty (diagonal-only) grid.

Step 5. The actual numbers — one cell, by hand

Concept is nice; let's grind real numbers. Shrink it right down: 5 readings of just 2 channels (A and B). We'll compute their one covariance cell. The same recipe scales straight up to 500×20.

① Subtract each column's mean → deviations (above/below center):

chA − 4 :  −2 ,  0 ,  +2 , −1 , +1
chB − 5 :  −2 ,  0 ,  +2 , −1 , +1

② Multiply the paired deviations, reading by reading:

(−2)(−2)=4 ,  (0)(0)=0 ,  (+2)(+2)=4 ,  (−1)(−1)=1 ,  (+1)(+1)=1
sum = 4+0+4+1+1 = 10

③ Average (divide by n−1 = 4):

cov(A,B) = 10 / 4 = 2.5   → positive → A & B move together ✓

That single number 2.5 fills one cell of the grid — position [A,B]. Repeat the exact same 3 steps for every channel pair to fill the whole matrix.

🔮 Notice: the 5 readings got summed and averaged into one number — they vanished. Only the pair of channels survives as a grid position.

Step 6. Deducing the grid size (500×20 → ? and 1000×20 → ?)

The matrix size is decided by one thing only: the number of channels — never the number of readings.

grid size = (# channels) × (# channels)
data shape# readings# channelscovariance matrix
500 × 205002020 × 20
1000 × 2010002020 × 20 (same!)
1000 × 8100088 × 8

Going 500 → 1000 readings does not change the grid — still 20×20. More readings only make each cell's average more trustworthy (steadier), like a latency percentile over 10,000 IOs vs 10.

How many channel-pairs is that, really?

For n = 20 channels the full grid has n × n = 400 cells. But it's a mirror (cov(A,B)=cov(B,A)), so you only ever compute:

diagonal (each channel with itself = its variance) :  n  = 20
distinct off-diagonal pairs :  n(n−1)/2 = 20·19/2 = 190
─────────────────────────────
unique numbers to compute :  n(n+1)/2 = 210  (the other 190 are copies)
🔮 Your turn: for 8 channels, how many distinct off-diagonal pairs? 8·7/2 = 28 pairs, + 8 variances on the diagonal = 36 unique numbers, in a 8×8 = 64-cell grid.

The shape flow (matrix form)

In one line of NumPy it's cov = Xc.T @ Xc / (n−1). Watch the dimensions — your inner-dimension rule:

Xc.T   @   Xc
(20 × 500) @ (500 × 20)  →  (20 × 20)
      ↑___________↑
inner 500 matches → gets summed away (the readings vanish)
outer 20 & 20 survive → the grid

Same with 1000 readings: (20×1000)@(1000×20) → 20×20. The 1000 cancels in the middle; the 20s stay.

Step 8. Eigen-decomposition — find the cigar's axes

Same move as the 6-student cigar, just in 20-D. We ask the covariance matrix for its eigenvectors (the directions the data-cloud stretches) and eigenvalues (how far it stretches each way).

evals, evecs = np.linalg.eig(cov)  # 20 of each, then sort big→small

eigenvector = a direction in 20-D space  ·  eigenvalue λ = how much the cloud spreads along it = how much information lives there
🔮 Predict before the chart: the data was built from 3 drivers. So how many of the 20 eigenvalues do you expect to be big, and how many near zero? 3 big, 17 ≈ 0. Next step proves it.

Step 9. The scree plot — 3 tall bars, then a cliff

Each bar = one eigenvalue (information in that direction), sorted tallest first. (animates when this step opens)

Read it: directions 1, 2, 3 are huge. Directions 4–20 are flat against the floor — those are the cigar's paper-thin "width," holding almost no signal (just the tiny sensor noise).

λ1 = 21.8  (50.7%)
λ2 = 13.2  (30.8%)
λ3 = 7.9   (18.4%)
λ4 … λ20 ≈ 0.003 each  (~0% each)

Step 10. Cumulative variance — how few can I keep?

Add the bars up left-to-right: how much of the total information do I capture as I keep more directions?

The curve rockets up, then goes flat: keep 1 → 50.7%, keep 2 → 81.5%, keep 3 → 99.9%. Keeping the 4th through 20th adds almost nothing.

Cross the 95% line and you hit it at exactly 3 directions. That's your answer: 3 numbers reconstruct ~99.9% of what all 20 sensors were telling you.

Step 12. The payoff — and this is your world

This whole flow is PCA: covariance → eigen → drop tiny-λ directions, keep the big ones.

per reading
→

+ eigen

3 numbers
~99% kept
Edge-AI win: store/process 3 features instead of 20 → ⅞ less SRAM, ⅞ fewer MACs, lower power — same signal. It's lossy compression for features, the same instinct you already have for NAND/bytes.
⚖️ When PCA BETRAYS you (the trade-off you always ask for):
  • The thin directions weren't noise. A rare thermal-runaway spike might live in a tiny-λ direction — PCA throws it away. Bad when the outlier is the thing you care about (anomaly detection).
  • You lose interpretability. The 3 kept numbers are blends of sensors, not "temperature" anymore. Hard to explain to a firmware reviewer.
  • Cost to compute. The covariance+eigen step itself costs MACs. Worth it only when you reuse the projection many times.
Rule: PCA wins when channels are redundant & you want compact features. It loses when every channel is independent, or the rare signal hides in a small direction.
