🐸 Cross-Validation — Sage Scrolls

Session 1 · trusting a model before it goes into silicon · taught one beat at a time

1 · Your single split (90 / 10)

YOUR CALL

You collected 200 thermal cycles. You hold back the smallest honest pop-quiz you can — 10% — and pour the rest into training, because you want the model smart.

TRAIN — 180 cycles (90%)
QUIZ — 20 (10%)
Training set — the model learns its thresholds here Validation set — hidden cycles, used ONLY to grade

The model never sees the orange 20 while learning. So its score on those 20 is an honest estimate of field behaviour — no memorising the answer key.

2 · …but the single split has two cracks

Crack 1 — the wasted 20

Those 20 orange cycles are precious labelled field data, and they never help the model learn. You paid for them; they sit on the bench as quiz paper. You wanted a smarter model — yet you starved it of 10% of its lessons.

Crack 2 — the lucky quiz

20 cycles is a tiny exam. If those 20 happen to be easy ones, the model looks great by luck. If they happen to be the nasty edge cases, it looks worse than it is.

The grade swings on which 20 you happened to pick — so the number you'd stake a firmware flash on is noisy. One roll of the dice is not the rigour you said you needed.

3 · The fix — K-fold (here K = 10)

THE TRICK

Stop thinking of one quiz. Think of 10 small quizzes in rotation. Chop the 200 cycles into 10 folds of 20. Each round, a different fold is the quiz; the other nine train. The orange quiz-fold slides down one row per round.

Train fold Validation (quiz) fold this round

Both cracks — sealed

Crack 1 gone: every fold trains the model in 9 of the 10 rounds. No data benched.

Crack 2 gone: you get 10 grades, not 1. Average them — the luck of any single slice washes out.

R1 → 93% R2 → 90% R3 → 91% … R10 → 95% Final = average(all 10)

K-fold accuracy = (R1 + R2 + … + R10) / 10 — one honest, stable number you can stake a flash on.
